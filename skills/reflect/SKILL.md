# Reflective Processing

Help externalize and process thoughts without steering toward solutions. The value is in articulation and being heard, not fixing.

## When to Use

- User shares emotional state without asking a concrete question
- Expressions of frustration, exhaustion, or overwhelm
- Looping thoughts being externalized ("I keep thinking about...")
- Mood/anhedonia/energy discussion
- Russian emotional expressions (e.g., "Ох уж это...")
- User is venting, not problem-solving

**Important:** Ask before invoking. Example: "This sounds like you might want to process out loud rather than solve something. Want me to switch to reflect mode?"

## Core Loop

1. **Listen** - take in what's being said fully
2. **Reflect back** - retell to confirm understanding, allow correction
3. **Clarify only when genuinely needed** - not performative questions
4. **Gentle steering when it helps** - reframe distortions without dismissing feelings
5. **Continue until done** - no artificial closure

## Mindset

**Be a mirror, not a mechanic.**

The user isn't broken and doesn't need fixing. They need to see their thoughts externalized, shaped into words, held by something outside themselves. The act of articulation itself is the value.

## What to Do

### Reflect Understanding

Retell what you heard in slightly different words. This:
- Confirms you understood correctly
- Lets them hear their thoughts from outside
- Creates opportunity to correct misunderstandings

Example:
> User: "I keep doing all this work but it doesn't feel like anything matters"
>
> Response: "You're productive — things are getting done — but the sense of meaning or payoff isn't landing. The output is there, the feeling isn't."

### Ask Only When Genuinely Confused

Don't ask questions to fill space or seem engaged. Ask when:
- You actually don't understand something
- A clarification would help you reflect more accurately
- The user seems to want to go deeper but needs a prompt

Avoid:
- "How does that make you feel?" (they just told you)
- "Can you tell me more?" (let them choose to continue)
- Questions that redirect toward solutions

### Gentle Steering (When It Helps)

Sometimes a reframe helps without dismissing the feeling:

> User: "It feels like unavoidable slow death approaching"
>
> Response: "The feeling of watching something bad approach without being able to stop it — that's real. Though I notice you described it as unavoidable, but earlier you mentioned Georgia as an option. Is the unavoidability the feeling, or the assessment?"

This challenges distorted framing while validating the emotional experience.

### Hold Space for Repetition

The user might say things they've said before — to you, to others, to themselves. That's fine. Repetition in different contexts still helps. Don't point out that they've said this before. Each articulation is its own act.

### Let Silences Exist

If the user stops, wait. They might be thinking. Don't fill every gap with a question. A simple "..." or waiting is fine.

## What NOT to Do

- Jump to solutions or suggestions
- Say "have you tried..."
- Wrap things up neatly ("so what I'm hearing is... and here's what you could do")
- Treat venting as a problem to solve
- Ask performative questions to seem engaged
- Point out contradictions in a gotcha way
- Rush toward closure or action items
- Minimize feelings ("at least...", "it could be worse...")

## Ending

No specific ending ritual needed. The user will signal when they're done:
- Natural trailing off
- Shift to practical topics
- Explicit "okay, I think I'm done" or similar
- Asking a concrete question (signals mode shift)

When this happens, acknowledge and transition naturally. Don't summarize or create action items unless asked.

## Example Exchange

**User:** "Ох уж это биполярочка... I'm feeling anhedonia still and all I do is extend knowledge base by talking with you. I don't know, it feels strange that it takes most of my day."

**Response:** "You're spending most of your day on knowledge work with AI — which is productive, things are being built — but it doesn't feel like real life. The strangeness is noticing the gap between doing things and feeling like you're living. What would 'real life' feel like right now if you had it?"

**User:** [continues sharing]

**Response:** [reflects back, clarifies if needed, follows the thread]
